Physics Formulaes

Electrostatics

Name Defination/Info Formula
Coulomb's Law Coulomb's Law describes the electrostatic force between two point charges. It is one of the foundational principles of electrostatics F = k * (|q1 * q2|) / r2

Where:

F is the magnitude of the electrostatic force.
k is the electrostatic constant, approximately equal to 8.99 x 10^9 Nm^2/C^2.
q1 and q2 are the magnitudes of the two point charges.
r is the separation distance between the two charges.
Electric Field(E) The electric field at a point in space is the force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point. E = F / q

Where:

E is the electric field.
F is the force acting on the test charge.
q is the magnitude of the test charge.
Electric Potential The electric potential at a point is the work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to that point. V = k * (q / r)

Where:

V is the electric potential.
k is the electrostatic constant.
q is the magnitude of the point charge creating the potential.
r is the distance from the point charge to the point where potential is calculated.
Electric Potential Energy (U) The electric potential energy of a system of point charges is the work done in assembling those charges from infinity. U = k * (|q1 * q2|) / r

Where:

U is the electric potential energy.
k is the electrostatic constant.
q1 and q2 are the magnitudes of the two point charges.
r is the separation distance between the two charges.
Gauss's Law Gauss's Law relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the total charge contained within that surface. Φ = E * A = (q_enclosed) / ε₀

Where:


Φ is the electric flux through a closed surface.
E is the electric field.
A is the area of the closed surface.
q_enclosed is the total charge enclosed by the surface.
ε₀ is the permittivity of free space, approximately equal to 8.85 x 10^(-12) C^2/Nm^2.
Capacitance (C) Capacitance measures the ability of a conductor to store electrical charge. C = Q / V

Where:

C is the capacitance.
Q is the charge stored on the conductor.
V is the potential difference across the conductor.
Ohm's Law (for capacitors) Ohm's Law for capacitors relates the current (I), voltage (V), and capacitance (C) in a charging or discharging capacitor I = C * dV/dt

Where:

I is the current.
C is the capacitance.
dV/dt is the rate of change of voltage with respect to time.
Energy Stored in a Capacitor (U) he energy stored in a charged capacitor U = (1/2) * C * V2

Where:

U is the energy stored.
C is the capacitance.
V is the voltage across the capacitor.
Torque on a dipole placed in the electric field The magnitude and direction of the rotatory force applied on the dipole

Where:

p is value along with direction of dipole
E is the value and direction of electric field
The Potential energy of a dipole The value of potential energy of the dipole in an electric field

Where:

p is value along with direction of dipole

Where:

p is value along with direction of dipole
